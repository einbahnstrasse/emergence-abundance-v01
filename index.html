Orchestration

2 paradigms:
  1. historical, syntactical
    • Served Western music for generations.
    • Primarily useful for orchestrating pitched musical materials, but does not properly solve for the presence of noise in instrumental settings.
    • As a reminder, white noise entered the orchestra rather late in the form of non-pitched percussion imitating Ottoman-era Janissary bands (e.g., W.A. Mozart, Abduction from the Seraglio, 1782)
    • Naively, we still ascribe the wrong terms to the effects we perceive. For example, ‚Äúspacing‚Äù fails to account for any of an aggregate‚Äôs basic timbral properties.

Orchestration

2 paradigms:
  1. historical, syntactical
  2. target-based, algorithmic
    • Uses heavy computation to find instrumental combinations that are ‚Äúbest fit‚Äù to match the specific, measurable qualities of ‚Äútarget‚Äù source sounds (e.g., ambient field recordings imitated within the orchestra).
    • Especially well suited to resolve noisier or complex and abstract sound sources.

Economics of Abundance

  • How can technology and innovation solve today‚Äôs global crises in housing and clean energy?
  • ‚ÄúTo have the future we want, we need to build and invent more of what we need.‚Äù Challenges a prevailing left-leaning and right-coded skepticism around supply-side planning.
  • ‚ÄúScarcity is a choice.‚Äù Against scarcity, austerity, and zero-sum thinking. Highlights policy failures that result not from supply limitations but from demand-side shortfalls: failures to distribute resources equitably or efficiently, or to overcome systemic inefficiencies.

Creative Abundance

  • Rejecting scarcity narratives: Abundance‚Äôs central premise aligns with my reluctance to accept gatekeeping and zero-sum thinking in musical culture. Institutions, including granters and academies funded (even partially) with public money, should be more transparent in their procedures.
  • Redistributing access: We shouldn‚Äôt limit our analysis to systems of redistribution, or reallocating resources, to correct for inequalities. These are all necessary and good, but they are short-sighted and only target demand-side incentivizing.
  • Individuals and institutions often perform their support for a progressive agenda without fundamentally altering power structures or working to expand opportunities for artists across a full continuum of representation.

Expanding the Pie

  • means arts organizations, presenters, ensembles, and other institutions should prioritize and redirect at least some (more) current resources towards workshops, ‚Äúlab work,‚Äù readings, and measurable artistic research & collaboration phases ‚Äî the very ‚Äúresearch & development‚Äù of new music ‚Äî in addition to commissioning.
  • does not mean funneling earmarked resources to the same channels for consumption by in-groups, by endlessly credentializing one another, or through ‚Äúmesearch.‚Äù These do not aim at expanding opportunity. This feedback loop perpetuates scarcity narratives that keep us believing resources will only ever be finite and available to an elite few ‚Äî as goalposts for entry are continually shifted.
  • Expansionary efforts foster understanding among producers of culture and create space for unlikely out-group participants, such as musicians from alternatively trained backgrounds, sound artists, and models of musicking we cannot currently imagine.

What We Can Change

  • Embracing exponential thinking: I explore how technologies and aesthetic juxtaposition can expand compositional horizons rather than merely replicate entrenched legacies.
  • We must learn to recognize how interactions inside our own community can reinforce scarcity and shape austerity policies imposed on us from above. My pieces sometimes confront this and related themes.

What We Can Change

  • We must stop villainizing contrarian & heterodox thinkers within our community while lionizing status quo symbols as inevitabilities or universalities.
  • Embrace a stance that includes composers among the primary stakeholders in a cultural funding ecosystem ‚Äî because these systems exist to support our creative labor. Most composers are too often excluded from decisions about how funds are distributed, perpetuating inequities in who gets supported and sustained. Composers are not passively consuming a luxury good...
  • Push our institutions to adopt more transparent measures: they should be more open and accountable for their decisions and outcomes, including more publicly available impact reporting.

Transparency

  • Institutions and gatekeepers should be more transparent if what we want is efficient, fast, more equitable, diverse, and overall more meaningful change. Summary

  • To observers, many arts organizations operate as private clubs, yet are often subsidized with tax exempt status, which problematizes accountability and oversight. Tax exemptions and grants represent forgone public revenue.
  • This contradiction creates an ethical dilemma: institutions operating with public subsidies must not shield decision-making behind private-sector opacity.
  • The public ‚Äî including artists seeking to access these resources ‚Äî bears the burden of non-disclosure, both in lost tax revenue and in denied opportunity, equity, and accountability.
  • This is not a right-coded call to dismantle public investment in the arts! But it is an appeal to ensure those investments actually foster equity, access, and public trust, not exclusion or unexamined privilege. Other countries often have stricter guidelines and consequences for opaqueness.
